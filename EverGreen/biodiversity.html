Life in the City

Biodiversity in Yangon

Understanding the web of life that trees support — and why protecting biodiversity is essential for a healthy city.

Web of Life

What Is Biodiversity?

Biodiversity — short for "biological diversity" — refers to the variety of all living things in an area, including plants, animals, insects, fungi, and microorganisms, and the ecosystems they form together. A place with high biodiversity has many different species living and interacting in a balanced way. A place with low biodiversity has few species and a fragile ecosystem.

Think of biodiversity like a tower made of building blocks. Each species is a block. The more blocks you have, the taller and stronger the tower becomes. If you start removing blocks — losing species — the tower becomes weaker and less stable. Remove too many blocks, and the whole tower collapses. That is what is happening in Yangon as green spaces disappear and species lose their habitats.

Why It Matters

Why Biodiversity Matters

🫁

Clean Air and Water

Plants and trees filter pollutants from the air and water. Wetlands clean water naturally by trapping sediments and breaking down harmful chemicals. Without biodiversity, we would need expensive artificial systems to do the same work.

🌾

Food Security

About one-third of the food we eat depends on pollinators like bees, butterflies, and bats. Without biodiversity, many of our fruits, vegetables, and nuts would not exist. Urban biodiversity supports pollinators that help feed the city.

💊

Medicine and Health

Many medicines come from plants and other organisms. Biodiversity is a treasure trove of potential cures and treatments. Losing species means losing potential medicines that could save lives in the future.

🛡️

Ecosystem Resilience

Ecosystems with high biodiversity are more resilient — they can recover from disasters like floods, droughts, and diseases more quickly. Monocultures (areas with only one species) are fragile and vulnerable to collapse.

🌳 Trees and Wildlife

Trees are the foundation of urban biodiversity. A single mature tree can support hundreds of species — from the birds that nest in its branches, to the insects that feed on its leaves, to the fungi that break down its fallen leaves on the ground.

Trees provide food in the form of fruits, seeds, nectar, and leaves. They provide shelter in the form of hollows, branches, and bark crevices. They create microclimates — cooler, moister areas that support plants and animals that could not survive in open, sun-baked areas.

In Yangon, large rain trees, banyan trees, and mango trees are especially important for wildlife. A single rain tree can support dozens of bird species, countless insects, epiphytic plants, and even small mammals.

🌳 Trees as Habitat

🐦 Birds in Urban Areas

Birds are among the most visible and beloved wildlife in cities. Yangon is home to a remarkable variety of birds — from the common house sparrow and myna to colorful kingfishers, elegant egrets, and majestic kites soaring over the city's lakes.

Birds play crucial roles in urban ecosystems. They control insect populations, pollinate flowers, disperse seeds, and serve as indicators of environmental health. When bird populations decline, it is often a warning sign that the broader ecosystem is in trouble.

Urban trees and green spaces are essential for Yangon's birds. Trees provide nesting sites, food sources, and safe resting places during migration. Without trees, many bird species would disappear from the city entirely.

🐦 Urban Birds

🐝 Pollinators

Pollinators — including bees, butterflies, moths, beetles, and bats — are essential for plant reproduction and food production. Without pollinators, many plants cannot produce fruits and seeds, and entire ecosystems can collapse.

In urban areas, pollinators depend on flowers, trees, and green spaces for food and habitat. When cities remove these resources, pollinator populations crash. This has been documented in cities around the world, where bee and butterfly populations have declined dramatically.

Yangon can support pollinators by planting flowering trees and native plants, creating community gardens, reducing pesticide use, and preserving existing green spaces. Even small actions — like planting a flowering tree in a schoolyard — can help pollinators survive and thrive in the city.

Local Species

Native Plants of Yangon

🌳

Rain Tree

A large shade tree with a wide canopy, common in Yangon's parks and roadsides. Supports many bird and insect species.

🌳

Banyan Tree

A sacred tree in Myanmar culture with aerial roots that create habitat for wildlife. Can support entire ecosystems.

🥭

Mango Tree

Provides food for people and wildlife, excellent shade, and cultural significance. One of Myanmar's most important trees.

🌸

Padauk Tree

Myanmar's national tree, prized for its beautiful golden flowers and valuable timber. Symbol of strength and resilience.

🔄 Healthy Ecosystems Need All Their Parts

In a healthy ecosystem, every organism has a role. Trees produce oxygen and provide habitat. Insects pollinate plants and decompose organic matter. Birds control insect populations and spread seeds. Fungi break down dead material and recycle nutrients. When we protect biodiversity, we protect the natural systems that sustain all life — including ours. This is why planting more trees in Yangon is not just about beautifying the city. It is about rebuilding the ecosystems that make the city livable.
